What is a Chiller?

A Chiller is a machine that removes heat from a liquid (typically water or water-glycol mixture) via a vapor-compression or absorption refrigeration cycle. This chilled liquid can then circulate through heat exchangers to cool equipment or spaces.

Key Functions of Chillers in Data Centers:

  • Heat Removal: Extract heat from IT equipment via cooling systems
  • Temperature Control: Maintain precise cooling water temperatures
  • Efficiency: Provide cooling more efficiently than direct expansion systems
  • Scalability: Support large cooling loads and facility expansion
  • Flexibility: Enable various cooling distribution strategies

The Refrigeration Cycle: Heat absorbed at evaporator, rejected at condenser

Refrigeration Cycle Basics:

  • Evaporator: Absorbs heat from chilled water (cooling load)
  • Compressor: Increases refrigerant pressure and temperature
  • Condenser: Rejects heat to cooling tower water or air
  • Expansion Valve: Reduces pressure, enabling cooling

Types of Chillers

Data centers typically use different types of chillers based on capacity, efficiency requirements, and site conditions.

1. By Cooling Method:

Air-Cooled Chillers

  • Heat Rejection: Direct to ambient air via fans
  • Capacity Range: 20-500 tons typical
  • Advantages: No cooling towers, lower water usage
  • Disadvantages: Lower efficiency, limited by ambient temperature
  • Application: Smaller facilities, water-scarce locations

Water-Cooled Chillers

  • Heat Rejection: To cooling tower water
  • Capacity Range: 100-3000+ tons
  • Advantages: Higher efficiency, larger capacities
  • Disadvantages: Requires cooling towers, water treatment
  • Application: Large data centers, efficiency focus

3. Advanced Chiller Technologies:

Variable Speed Drive (VSD) Chillers

  • Compressor speed varies with load demand
  • Significant energy savings at part loads
  • Better capacity control and staging
  • Reduced mechanical stress and wear
  • Premium efficiency: 0.45-0.55 kW/ton at full load

Critical Chiller Components

1. Evaporator

Function and Features:

  • Purpose: Transfer heat from chilled water to refrigerant
  • Types: Shell-and-tube, plate-and-frame
  • Temperature: Produces 42-45¬∞F chilled water typically
  • Approach: 1-2¬∞F between water and refrigerant
  • Protection: Freeze protection and flow switches

2. Compressor Types

Centrifugal Compressors:

  • Operation: High-speed impeller creates pressure
  • Capacity Control: Inlet guide vanes or VSD
  • Oil-Free: Magnetic or air bearings available
  • Efficiency: Best at full load operation
  • Maintenance: Lower maintenance requirements

3. Condenser

  • Shell-and-Tube: Water flows through tubes
  • Air-Cooled: Finned coils with fans
  • Approach Temperature: 5-7¬∞F to entering water
  • Fouling Factor: Design for maintenance
  • Relief Devices: Pressure relief valves required

Chiller Plant Design and Configuration

Data center chiller plants must be designed for reliability, efficiency, and scalability.

1. Primary-Secondary Configuration

  • Primary Loop: Constant flow through chillers
  • Secondary Loop: Variable flow to loads
  • Decoupler: Hydraulic separation between loops
  • Benefits: Chiller protection, flexible operation
  • Application: Traditional design, proven reliability

2. Variable Primary Flow

  • Single Loop: Variable flow through chillers and loads
  • Efficiency: Eliminates secondary pumps
  • Control: Requires minimum flow management
  • Savings: Reduced pumping energy
  • Trend: Modern best practice for efficiency

  • Efficiency Optimization: Run most efficient combination
  • Runtime Equalization: Balance operating hours
  • Soft Loading: Gradual load application
  • Anti-Cycling: Minimum on/off timers

Efficiency and Performance

Understanding and optimizing chiller efficiency is crucial for data center operating costs and sustainability.

1. Efficiency Metrics

Key Performance Indicators:

  • kW/ton: Power per ton of cooling (lower is better)
  • COP: Coefficient of Performance (higher is better)
  • IPLV: Integrated Part Load Value (weighted efficiency)
  • NPLV: Non-standard Part Load Value
  • Annual kWh/ton: Total energy per cooling delivered

2. Typical Efficiency Ranges

Chiller Type Full Load (kW/ton) IPLV (kW/ton) Best-in-Class
Air-Cooled 1.0-1.2 0.85-1.0 <0.90
Water-Cooled Screw 0.65-0.75 0.50-0.60 <0.50
Water-Cooled Centrifugal 0.55-0.65 0.40-0.50 <0.40
Magnetic Bearing 0.45-0.55 0.35-0.45 <0.35

3. Factors Affecting Efficiency

Operating Conditions Impact:

  • Lift: Temperature difference between evaporator and condenser
  • Load: Chillers most efficient at 70-80% capacity
  • Condenser Water Temp: Lower is better (1¬∞F = 1.5% efficiency)
  • Chilled Water Temp: Higher is better (1¬∞F = 2% efficiency)
  • Fouling: Tube cleaning critical for performance

4. Optimization Strategies

Best Practices for Efficiency:

  • Free Cooling: Use when ambient allows
  • Chilled Water Reset: Raise temperature when possible
  • Condenser Water Reset: Lower temperature in cool weather
  • Optimal Staging: Run chillers at sweet spot
  • VSD Application: Match speed to load
  • All-Variable Speed: Chillers, pumps, and towers

5. Performance Monitoring

  • Real-Time Efficiency: Track kW/ton continuously
  • Trend Analysis: Identify degradation early
  • Benchmarking: Compare to design and peers
  • Diagnostics: Identify optimization opportunities
  • Reporting: Dashboard for operations team

2. Critical Maintenance Tasks

Tube Maintenance:

  • Cleaning: Annual mechanical or chemical cleaning
  • Inspection: Eddy current testing for tube integrity
  • Water Treatment: Prevent scale and corrosion
  • Approach Temperature: Monitor for fouling indication
  • Performance Impact: 10% efficiency loss possible

3. Oil Analysis

  • Moisture Content: <50 ppm target
  • Acid Number: Indicates oil breakdown
  • Metal Content: Wear indication
  • Viscosity: Oil condition
  • Refrigerant Content: Seal condition

4. Common Issues and Troubleshooting

Typical Problems:

  • High Head Pressure: Dirty condenser, non-condensables
  • Low Suction Pressure: Low refrigerant, restricted flow
  • Surge: Low load, high lift conditions
  • High Oil Temperature: Low refrigerant, bearing wear
  • Vibration: Misalignment, bearing issues

5. Reliability Best Practices

Maximizing Uptime:

  • Predictive Maintenance: Vibration and oil trending
  • Spare Parts: Critical components in stock
  • Training: Operator certification programs
  • Documentation: Detailed logs and history
  • Service Contracts: OEM or qualified providers
  • Remote Monitoring: 24/7 expert oversight
